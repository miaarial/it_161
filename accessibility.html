Accessibility

 Accessibility is key to creating a website for the broadest audience possible, as well as showing an enthusiastically empathetic approach to the needs of more than a quarter of people in the United States.

According to the CDC 26% of adults in the United States have some type of disability.

  • 10.8% experience difficulties with cognition and memory.
  • 5.9% are hard of hearing in some way.
  • 4.6% have difficulty seeing in some way.

 This is only what is reported, and there are many other instances where having an accessible web page would be beneficial to a multitude of internet users.

 This video highlights the basics of accessible web design, and the many people it benefits.

 The goal of any website should be the ability to be perceived by all users in every way the website has to offer, and the ability for all users to contribute if they so desire. Web developers do have to go out of their way to accommodate, but it is definitely worth it to have the broadest audience possible, as well as to show all users that there is some empathy behind the screen.

 One of the best tools in any web developer’s tool belt is the W3, and their guidelines on accessibility (WCAG - Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) are an invaluable tool for creating websites that are accessible to everybody.

 The main WCAG document is a bit tedious, but is broken down into the different types of content a web developer will need to make accessible:

Perceivable content:

  • Use of color
  • Pictures
  • Video

Operable content:

  • Navigation
  • Keyboard accessibility

Understandable content:

  • Readability
  • Predictability
  • Input assistance

This makes it easier to research specific elements and tags, as well as to deep dive into the topic of accessibility in one place.

 While every aspect of accessibility is equally important, I think it can be useful to point beginning web developers in the right direction as far as where to start their accessibility journey. The <title> tag, <caption> tag, and “alt” attribute are easy to apply to any website, and make a world of difference. All three of these elements will provide a way for screen readers to get information to internet users who are blind or visually impaired, which is a large chunk of the population! Additionally, users who are deaf and hard of hearing will greatly appreciate captions on videos, and transcripts to podcasts, as well as many people with cognitive disabilities such as those with dyslexia and ADHD.

 Accessibility is not a burden to add to your work as a web developer, but simply a part of the job. It is the obvious right thing to do to add accessibility functions to every webpage, and people with and without disabilities will be appreciative of the additional effort.
